# UK vehicle and mileage expenses — guide

How you claim depends on three factors: are you a sole trader or Ltd? Is the vehicle owned by you personally or by the business? And which method do you choose — simplified mileage or actual costs?

## Decision tree

| Setup | Best method (usually) |
|---|---|
| Sole trader, owns vehicle personally | **Simplified mileage** (45p/25p). Easiest, no capital allowances, no fuel records. |
| Sole trader, vehicle ≥ 50% business use, willing to keep records | **Actual costs** with apportionment can sometimes beat simplified mileage. |
| Limited company director driving own car for business | **Mileage allowance** at 45p/25p paid tax-free by company. Don't put a personal car through the company's books. |
| Limited company, dedicated commercial vehicle (van) | **Company-owned**, actual costs through company, capital allowances on purchase. |
| Limited company, dedicated company car | **Company-owned with BIK implications** — see below. Usually only makes sense for EVs given current BIK rates. |

## Simplified mileage (Approved Mileage Allowance Payments — AMAP)

The rates published by HMRC. Paid tax-free; covers all running costs (fuel, insurance, repairs, depreciation).

| Vehicle | First 10,000 business miles/year | Above 10,000 business miles/year |
|---|---|---|
| Car or van | 45p per mile | 25p per mile |
| Motorcycle | 24p per mile | 24p per mile |
| Bicycle | 20p per mile | 20p per mile |
| Passenger (per additional business passenger sharing the journey) | +5p per mile | +5p per mile |

Worked example — sole trader driving 12,000 business miles in the year:
- First 10,000 miles × 45p = £4,500
- Next 2,000 miles × 25p = £500
- Total claim: £5,000

What "business mile" means:
- Home to a temporary workplace ✓
- Between two business locations ✓
- Client visits, supplier visits, training that's part of existing trade ✓
- Home to a regular workplace ✗ (ordinary commuting)
- Personal errand on the way back from a business meeting — split the trip

You **cannot** also claim fuel, insurance, repairs, or capital allowances on top of simplified mileage. The 45p/25p covers all of that.

## Actual cost method

Track actual vehicle expenses and apportion by business use percentage.

Allowable costs (in proportion):
- Fuel
- Insurance
- Road tax
- MOT
- Repairs and servicing
- AA/RAC subscription
- Interest on a vehicle finance agreement

PLUS **capital allowances** on the cost of the vehicle. For a sole trader using a car partly for business:
- The vehicle's "qualifying expenditure" is the cost × business use %
- Cars (not vans) attract WDA based on CO₂ emissions:
  - 0g/km (pure EV): 100% first-year allowance (for new electric cars)
  - 1-50g/km: 18% main pool WDA
  - Over 50g/km: 6% special-rate WDA (slow write-down)
- Vans qualify for 100% Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) up to £1m

Worked example — sole trader, car cost £20,000, 60% business use, CO₂ 100g/km, actual annual running costs £4,000:
- Running cost claim: £4,000 × 60% = £2,400
- Capital allowance: (£20,000 × 60%) × 6% WDA = £720 in year 1
- Total deduction: £3,120

Compare to simplified mileage at, say, 8,000 business miles: 8,000 × 45p = £3,600.

In this case **simplified wins** — most users with mid-mileage and modest car costs get a better result from simplified mileage. Actual-cost wins for high-mileage users with cheap, well-maintained vehicles, or for users with expensive cars who'd struggle with the 25p rate above 10,000 miles.

You must pick one method **and stick with it for that vehicle**. Switching mid-life is not allowed; you can change methods when you change vehicles.

## Limited company directors using own car

The director drives their **personal** car for company business. The company pays them mileage at HMRC AMAP rates (45p / 25p / 24p / 20p). The payment is:

- Tax-free in the director's hands (up to AMAP rates).
- Deductible for the company as a business expense.

If the company pays MORE than AMAP, the excess is taxable on the director (Benefit in Kind). If the company pays LESS, the director can claim Mileage Allowance Relief on their Self Assessment for the shortfall — but only as a personal tax relief, not a refund of the mileage itself.

Most importantly: **do NOT put a personal car through the company's books** (insurance, fuel, repairs). The car isn't a company asset; treating it as one creates significant BIK issues.

## Company-owned car (BIK trap)

If the company buys a car for the director's use (including any private use, even occasional), it's a "company car" — taxable benefit in kind:

- BIK = list price × CO₂-based percentage (currently up to 37% for high-emission, as low as 2% for pure EVs).
- Income tax owed by the director on the BIK at their marginal rate.
- Class 1A NIC owed by the company at 13.8% on the BIK.
- Fuel benefit on top if the company pays for personal fuel.

For most petrol/diesel cars the BIK tax burden far exceeds any saving — **don't recommend** company cars for directors of close companies unless it's a fully electric vehicle.

EVs are currently 2-5% BIK rate, making them attractive — but verify the current rate before quoting; this has been creeping up.

## Vans

Vans are simpler than cars:
- Company-owned van for business use only: no BIK.
- Company van with insignificant private use (e.g. occasional travel to a tip): no BIK.
- Company van with material private use: flat BIK rate (~£3,960/year at last check, plus ~£757 for fuel) — much lower than equivalent car.
- Annual Investment Allowance of 100% on van purchase up to £1m.

## What the agent should ask

Before computing anything:
1. Sole trader or limited company?
2. Who owns the vehicle — you personally or the business?
3. Car, van, motorcycle, or bicycle?
4. Approximate business miles per year?
5. If car: CO₂ emissions or EV?
6. Have you used simplified or actual-cost in prior years on this vehicle?

The first two answers determine the *mechanism*. The rest determine the *numbers*.

## Common errors

- Claiming home-to-work commute as business miles.
- Mixing methods (claiming AMAP plus fuel receipts) — illegal.
- Claiming a passenger £5/mile add-on without an actual business passenger sharing the journey.
- Sole traders claiming both mileage AND capital allowances on the same car — pick one method.
- Putting a personal car's expenses through a limited company's books.
- Forgetting Class 1A NIC on company car benefits.
- Not adjusting capital allowance pool for private use percentage.
