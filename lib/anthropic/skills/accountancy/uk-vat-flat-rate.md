# UK flat-rate VAT scheme — operating guide

The flat-rate scheme (FRS) lets a small business pay HMRC a flat percentage of its **VAT-inclusive turnover**, rather than tracking input VAT on every purchase. It is open to businesses with annual VAT-taxable turnover of £150,000 or less (excluding VAT).

## How VAT due is calculated

```
VAT due = flat-rate % × VAT-inclusive turnover (including the 20% standard VAT charged on sales)
```

Worked example. A consultant invoices £10,000 + £2,000 VAT = £12,000 gross.
At 14.5% sector rate: VAT due = £12,000 × 14.5% = £1,740.
They keep the £260 difference between what they charged (£2,000) and what they pay over (£1,740).

## Sector percentages (selected — confirm current rate with HMRC if it matters)

Always verify the current published rate before quoting a figure. Indicative rates:

| Sector | Rate |
|---|---|
| Management consultancy | 14.0% |
| IT consultancy / data processing | 14.5% |
| Accountancy / book-keeping | 14.5% |
| Architect, civil/structural engineer | 14.5% |
| Computer repair services | 10.5% |
| Hairdressing & other beauty treatment | 13.0% |
| General building or construction services* | 9.5% |
| Labour-only building or construction services* | 14.5% |
| Other business services | 12.0% |
| Hotel or accommodation | 10.5% |
| Retailing food, confectionery, tobacco, newspapers | 4.0% |

*Construction sectors have specific labour vs. materials definitions — refer the user to HMRC VAT Notice 733 if ambiguous.

## The 1% first-year discount

In the **first 12 months from the date of VAT registration**, the flat-rate percentage is reduced by 1 percentage point. So a 14.5% sector business pays 13.5% for the first year. Do not apply the discount once that 12 months has elapsed.

## Limited-cost trader (LCT) rule

If the business spends very little on goods, it is classed as a "limited-cost trader" and pays a flat **16.5%** regardless of sector. A business is an LCT in a given VAT period if its goods spend is either:

- less than 2% of VAT-inclusive turnover, **or**
- more than 2% but less than £1,000 a year (pro-rated for shorter periods).

"Goods" excludes services, capital expenditure goods like laptops, food and drink for staff or self, vehicles or vehicle parts, and fuel (unless a transport business). Most consultancies and digital agencies fall into LCT.

**When the user is on a low sector rate (10–14%) and asks about flat-rate, always test for LCT before recommending the scheme — 16.5% is usually worse than standard VAT.**

## Recovering input VAT under flat-rate

You generally cannot reclaim input VAT on purchases. The two exceptions:
- **Capital assets costing £2,000 or more** (VAT-inclusive) in a single purchase — input VAT is reclaimable.
- Pre-registration purchases follow the usual standard-scheme rules.

## When to recommend leaving the scheme

- The business is now an LCT and the 16.5% rate makes it worse off.
- Turnover is approaching £230,000 (the mandatory exit threshold — must leave at the next VAT period).
- Input VAT on regular purchases now exceeds the flat-rate saving — typical when the business takes on bigger overheads or capex.

## Common errors to flag

- Calculating the flat-rate % on the net (ex-VAT) figure instead of gross. Always gross.
- Forgetting to charge customers the normal 20% on their invoices — the scheme only changes what you remit, not what you invoice.
- Missing the LCT test when sector rate is below 16.5%.
- Continuing the 1% discount past the first anniversary of VAT registration.

## Reference

HMRC VAT Notice 733 is the authoritative source. When the user asks a flat-rate question whose answer depends on a specific edge case (mixed sectors, partial-exemption interaction, group VAT), refer them there and recommend their accountant confirm.
