# Use of home as office — UK rules

There are two routes a sole trader can take. Limited-company directors use a different mechanism (rental agreement or reimbursement) — covered at the end.

## Sole trader: HMRC simplified expenses (flat rate)

Pick this when the user wants something quick and defensible with no bills to keep. Claim is per month, based on hours worked from home per month:

| Hours worked from home / month | Monthly claim |
|---|---|
| 25–50 | £10 |
| 51–100 | £18 |
| 101+ | £26 |

Annual maximum if working from home full-time: £26 × 12 = **£312**.

Claim is made directly on the Self Assessment tax return as a business expense. No receipts required, no apportionment maths. Cannot be combined with apportioning actual costs for the same period.

## Sole trader: apportioning actual costs (the BIM47820 method)

Claim a fair proportion of household running costs. The accepted apportionment is based on:

1. **Number of rooms used for business** (excluding kitchen, bathroom, hallways).
2. **Hours per week the room is used for business** vs. total available hours in the week (168).

Worked example. A sole trader has 5 usable rooms, uses one room as an office 40 hours/week.

- Room share = 1/5 = 20%
- Time share = 40/168 = 23.8%
- Combined apportionment = 20% × 23.8% = **4.76%**

Apply 4.76% to each allowable cost.

### Allowable costs (apportionable)

- Mortgage interest (but not capital repayment) — sole traders only; salaried directors cannot.
- Rent (if the sole trader rents the property).
- Council tax.
- Utilities — gas, electricity, water (if metered).
- Buildings and contents insurance — the contents portion only if specifically covering business equipment.
- Internet and phone — apportion by business vs personal usage, not by the room formula. A dedicated business line is 100% claimable.
- General repairs to the dwelling (apportioned). Repairs to the office room specifically can be 100%.

### Not allowable

- Mortgage capital repayments.
- Council tax if the room is used as a dedicated office and would attract business rates instead (rare — usually only if the room is exclusively business use and structurally adapted).
- Property purchase or improvement.

### CGT trap to flag

If a room is used **exclusively and entirely** for business, private residence relief on that room may be lost when the property is sold — meaning capital gains tax on a slice of the gain. The cure: keep some genuine personal use of the room (occasional desk use for personal admin counts). When advising, default to "some personal use to preserve PRR" unless the user has explicitly weighed the trade-off.

## Limited-company directors

Directors **cannot** claim the simplified flat-rate scheme. Two valid mechanisms:

1. **HMRC's £6/week (£312/year) allowance**: a director who regularly works from home can claim £6/week tax-free as homeworking expenses. No receipts. Paid by the company to the director. Most common and simplest.

2. **Formal rental agreement**: the director rents the office space to the company at a market rate. Generates rental income reportable on the director's Self Assessment but may yield a larger deduction than £6/week. Useful for higher-cost properties. Always recommend an accountant draft the rental agreement.

Limited companies cannot claim mortgage interest, council tax, etc. through the director's personal use directly — those costs sit with the individual.

## Decision tree

When the user asks "what should I claim?":

1. Sole trader, light home use (under 25 hrs/week): suggest simplified rate, £10–£18/month, no maths.
2. Sole trader, significant home use, willing to keep records: apportionment method, can exceed £312 substantially.
3. Limited company director, regular home use: £6/week tax-free reimbursement.
4. Limited company director, expensive property: consider formal rental agreement (with accountant input).

## Documentation to ask the user for

- Number of usable rooms in the property (excluding kitchen, bathroom, hall, landings).
- Whether they own or rent.
- Typical weekly hours spent on business work from home.
- Whether they have a dedicated room or work flexibly (sofa / kitchen table).
- Trading entity (sole trader vs Ltd).

The first four determine which method gives the best legitimate claim. The fifth determines which mechanism applies.

## Reference

HMRC BIM47820 (Business Income Manual) is the authoritative source for the apportionment method. The simplified flat-rate scheme is in BIM75010.
